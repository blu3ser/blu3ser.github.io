What is Electronic Warfare?

As David Adamy cites in his book EW 101: Electronic warfare is defined as the art and science of preserving the use of the electromagnetic spectrum for friendly use while denying its use to the enemy.

Electronic Warfare has historically been categorized into three main groups: Electronic Countermeasures (ECM), Electronic Support Measures (ESM), and Electronic Counter-Countermeasures (ECCM). However, NATO has recently revised the classification and based in the old classification stablish three new groups: Electronic Attack (EA), Electronic Protection (EP), and Electronic Support (ES). Here we will focus on the EA group, focusing specifically on jamming.

Jamming is a technique used to disrupt an enemy's ability to receive important information. It involves sending a strong interfering signal alongside the desired signal to overpower it. The effectiveness of jamming relies on the interfering signal being powerful enough to prevent the enemy from extracting the necessary information from the desired signal. This can occur when the jamming signal overwhelms the desired signal or when the combined signals have characteristics that make it difficult for a processor to extract the desired information.

The testing process

The extensive simulation capabilities of Command Modern Operations have been utilized for conducting these tests. The integration of the LUA language enables the automation of scenario creation and data collection processes. These tests were performed using the commercial version 1328.5 of the software. The test scenario comprises the following elements:

  • A radar positioned at an elevated point of 6814m, ensuring no line-of-sight interference.
  • An F-16A Falcon aircraft located at 1.5 times the maximum detection distance of the radar, positioned at 270 degrees relative to the radar and at an altitude of 10000m.
  • The presence of an electronic warfare aircraft, with its systems activated, aimed at disrupting the radar, depending on the specific scenario.

During the tests, the distance at which the test radar detected the F-16A aircraft was measured. This distance can be presented in two ways: normalized to the maximum detection range of the radar for comparing different radars, or in nautical miles as the actual distance.
